1. Core Concept & Energy Systems Overview
What are anaerobic adaptations?
▾

What are anaerobic adaptations?

Physiological and neuromuscular changes that improve the ability to produce high power rapidly without oxygen — at intensities above 85% VO₂max, lasting 0–2 minutes.

🔋 Energy Systems — they overlap, not separate

0–10 sec

ATP-PCr System

Explosive energy — phosphocreatine rapidly resynthesises ATP. No oxygen needed.

10 sec – 2 min

Glycolytic System

Rapid ATP from glycogen breakdown. Accumulates H⁺ → fatigue.

2+ min

Oxidative System

Recovery & sustained support. Clears lactate, restores PCr.

🔬
2. Metabolic Adaptations
Energy supply improvements
▾

What improves with anaerobic training:

↑ PCr Stores

Greater phosphocreatine availability for rapid ATP resynthesis.

↑ Glycolytic Enzymes

PFK, LDH activity increases → faster ATP from glycogen.

↑ Buffering Capacity

Better H⁺ tolerance → delayed fatigue during high-intensity work.

Outcome: Faster energy production + delayed fatigue onset
💪
3. Neuromuscular Adaptations
Force production & neural drive
▾
Neural Drive

🧠 Motor Unit Recruitment & EMG

  • ↑ Motor unit recruitment (especially Type II fibres)
  • ↑ Firing frequency (rate coding)
  • ↑ EMG amplitude → greater activation
👉 Early strength gains are neural — not hypertrophy
SSC

🔁 Stretch–Shortening Cycle

Phases: Eccentric (stretch) → Amortisation (transition) → Concentric (shortening)

Adaptations: ↓ Amortisation time, ↑ Elastic energy reuse, ↑ Rate of force development (RFD)

Reflex

⚡ Myotatic (Stretch) Reflex

Muscle spindles detect rapid stretch → reflex increases activation. Training effect: ↑ Reflex sensitivity, faster contraction.

👉 Critical for sprinting, jumping, change of direction

🏃 Plyometric Training

Develops SSC efficiency + reflex contribution + neural coordination → ↑ Reactive strength, ↑ RFD, ↑ movement efficiency

🧠 Coordination & Efficiency

↓ Antagonist co-activation, ↑ Intermuscular coordination → "Better movement = more effective force"

🧬
4. Muscle & Structural Adaptations
Fibre type & architecture changes
▾

💥 Fibre Type Adaptations

  • ↑ Type II fibre hypertrophy
  • Type IIx → IIa shift (more fatigue resistant)
  • Greater glycolytic capacity per fibre

📐 Muscle Architecture

  • ↑ Pennation angle → more fibres packed → ↑ force
  • ↑ Fascicle length → more sarcomeres in series → ↑ velocity
  • ↑ Tendon stiffness → faster force transmission + elastic recoil

6. Anaerobic Speed Reserve (ASR) & Individual Differences
Modern concept in high-intensity training
▾

What is ASR?

ASR = Max Sprint Speed − Max Aerobic Speed

Determines tolerance to high-intensity work. Better predictor than %VO₂max alone. Performance above MAS depends on % of ASR used.

Individual Differences

Athletes respond differently due to fibre type, neuromuscular profile, and ASR. ASR-based training reduces variability in fatigue and lactate response.
