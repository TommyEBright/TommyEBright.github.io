Qualitative
Phase Analysis.

How to observe and evaluate human movement systematically — using the overhead squat as a worked example.

← Back to Biomechanics Hub
Overview

Watching movement properly.

Qualitative analysis is the systematic observation and evaluation of movement quality without the use of numerical data. It is what every good coach, physiotherapist, and sport scientist does naturally when they watch an athlete move — but done well, it requires a structured approach, a clear framework, and a solid understanding of what normal and abnormal movement look like.

This sits in contrast to quantitative analysis, which uses instruments — cameras, force platforms, electromyography — to produce numerical data. Both approaches have value, but qualitative analysis is often the first tool a practitioner reaches for. It costs nothing, requires no equipment, and when done well, it reveals a great deal. Knudson's qualitative analysis model provides four stages: preparation, observation, evaluation and diagnosis, and intervention. This module focuses primarily on observation and evaluation.

The overhead squat is one of the most informative screening movements available to sport science practitioners. It loads multiple joints simultaneously and reveals compensatory movement patterns that would not be visible in simpler tests. The movement breaks naturally into two phases: the descent phase (from standing to the lowest position) and the ascent phase (return to standing). Each phase places different demands on the joints and muscles involved.

At the hip, the descent phase requires eccentric control from the gluteal muscles and hip extensors — the muscle fibres are lengthening under load to control the descent. During ascent, these same muscles work concentrically to drive extension. At the knee, the quadriceps control the rate of flexion eccentrically on the way down and produce extension concentrically on the way up. The ankle requires sufficient dorsiflexion range during descent — limited ankle mobility is one of the most common causes of the heel rising or the trunk pitching forward.

Understanding these mechanics makes error identification systematic rather than guesswork. Common errors include forward lean of the trunk (often caused by limited ankle mobility or weak hip extensors), valgus collapse at the knee (often associated with hip abductor weakness), and heel rise (almost always limited ankle dorsiflexion). Identifying the error is step one — the more important step is identifying its cause.

Movement Phases

Overhead squat — phase breakdown.

Descent Phase

From the standing start position to the lowest point. The hips, knees and ankles all flex simultaneously. Key muscles work eccentrically to control the rate of descent. The trunk should remain upright and the arms overhead throughout.

Eccentric ControlHip/Knee/Ankle FlexionTrunk Stability
Ascent Phase

From the lowest position back to standing. The hips, knees and ankles all extend. The same muscles that worked eccentrically on descent now work concentrically to drive the movement. The pattern of extension should mirror the descent.
