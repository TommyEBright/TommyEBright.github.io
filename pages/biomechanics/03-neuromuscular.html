The Neuromuscular
System.

Motor units, muscle fibre types, contraction types, and the stretch-shortening cycle explained through the lens of athletic performance.

← Back to Biomechanics Hub
Overview

How muscles produce force.

The neuromuscular system is the functional unit of movement. Understanding it bridges the gap between the biomechanical analysis of motion and the physiological mechanisms that produce it. At its core, movement is the result of electrical signals from the nervous system causing muscle fibres to contract — and the mechanical outcome depends enormously on which fibres are recruited, in what order, and at what rate.

A motor unit consists of a single motor neuron and all the muscle fibres it innervates. This is the smallest functional unit of force production. When a motor neuron fires, all of its muscle fibres contract simultaneously — this is the all-or-nothing principle. The force produced by a muscle is controlled by two mechanisms: recruitment (activating more motor units) and rate coding (increasing the firing frequency of active motor units). During low-force tasks, only a few small motor units are active. As demand increases, progressively larger units are recruited — a principle known as Henneman's size principle.

Human skeletal muscle contains a spectrum of fibre types. Type I (slow-twitch) fibres are fatigue-resistant, oxidative, and well-suited to sustained low-force activities. Type IIa fibres have intermediate properties — faster than Type I but more fatigue-resistant than Type IIx. Type IIx (fast-twitch) fibres produce the greatest force at the highest speed but fatigue rapidly. They are the dominant fibre type recruited during maximal sprinting, jumping, and throwing. Most humans have a roughly equal mix of Type I and Type II fibres in the large locomotor muscles, though this varies considerably between individuals and across muscles.

The three types of muscle contraction — concentric, eccentric, and isometric — describe the relationship between force production and length change. Concentric action involves the muscle shortening under load. Eccentric action involves the muscle lengthening under load — typically to control or decelerate a movement. Isometric action involves force production with no change in muscle length. In most sport movements, all three types occur in sequence.

The stretch-shortening cycle (SSC) is the mechanism that underpins most athletic movements. When a muscle is rapidly stretched (eccentric phase) immediately before a concentric action, the concentric output is enhanced — jump height is greater, sprint speed is faster. This occurs through the storage and return of elastic energy in the muscle-tendon unit, and the potentiation of muscle force via the stretch reflex. The countermovement jump is a classic SSC movement — the brief downward movement before take-off loads the system and increases jump height compared to a squat jump performed without a countermovement.
