Overview

What is biomechanics?

Biomechanics is the study of the mechanical principles that govern movement in living organisms. In sport and exercise science, it gives us the tools to describe, analyse, and improve the way people move — whether that's a sprinter accelerating out of the blocks, a golfer generating clubhead speed, or a patient recovering from a knee injury. It sits at the intersection of physics and biology, and it's one of the most practically useful disciplines in the sport science toolkit.

Within biomechanics, we work with two broad categories of analysis. Kinematics describes movement without reference to the forces that cause it — how fast something moves, in what direction, and how that changes over time. Kinetics is concerned with the forces that produce or result from movement — the push of the ground against a sprinter's foot, the torque generated at a joint, the impulse that sends a javelin into the air. Both are essential, and you'll use both throughout this module.

Before we can measure anything meaningfully, we need to agree on how we measure it. Sport biomechanics uses the International System of Units (SI) — metres for distance, seconds for time, kilograms for mass, newtons for force. Being consistent with units matters more than it might seem: a measurement without a unit is meaningless, and mixing units is one of the most common sources of error in lab work.

You'll also encounter the distinction between scalar and vector quantities early and often. Scalars have magnitude only — speed, distance, mass. Vectors have both magnitude and direction — velocity, displacement, force. This distinction matters practically: knowing a force is 500 N tells you something, but knowing it's 500 N directed vertically downward tells you much more.

Finally, it's worth reflecting on why measurement error matters. No measurement tool is perfect — force platforms have noise, cameras have lens distortion, digitising introduces human error. A good biomechanist understands where errors come from, how to minimise them, and how to report uncertainty honestly. This isn't just academic; in applied settings, acting on bad data can lead to poor decisions about training, rehabilitation, or technique.

Recommended Reading

Where to go next.

McGinnis, P. M. (2013). Biomechanics of sport and exercise (3rd ed.). Human Kinetics.
Chapter 1 — The core introductory text for this module. Broad, accessible, and well illustrated. Start here if you want a solid grounding in the fundamentals.
Bartlett, R. (2007). Introduction to sports biomechanics (2nd ed.). Routledge.
Chapter 1 — A slightly more analytical framing of the same material. Good if you want more depth or a second perspective on concepts you found tricky in McGinnis.
